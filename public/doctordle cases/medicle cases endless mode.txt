DIAGNOSIS
Cardiac amyloidosis

ALIASES
Amyloid cardiomyopathy
Infiltrative amyloid heart disease
Restrictive cardiomyopathy due to amyloidosis

VIGNETTE_LINES

1. A 68-year-old man presents with progressive shortness of breath.
2. He has had several months of bilateral hand numbness and underwent carpal tunnel release 2 years ago.
3. Examination shows elevated jugular venous pressure, bilateral lower-extremity edema, and clear lungs.
4. ECG demonstrates low-voltage QRS complexes, and basic metabolic panel shows mild renal insufficiency.
5. Echocardiography reveals concentric ventricular wall thickening with biatrial enlargement and a restrictive filling pattern despite a preserved ejection fraction.

TEACHING_POINTS

 Heart failure symptoms with increased ventricular wall thickness and unexpectedly low ECG voltage should suggest an infiltrative cardiomyopathy.
 Bilateral carpal tunnel syndrome and peripheral neuropathy can precede overt cardiac manifestations of amyloid disease.
 Restrictive physiology often presents with prominent right-sided congestion and relatively clear lungs early in the course.
 Tissue confirmation relies on identifying amyloid deposits with Congo red staining and characteristic birefringence.

Acetaminophen hepatotoxicity

VIGNETTE_LINES

1. A 20-year-old woman is brought in after taking a large amount of an over-the-counter pain reliever.
2. Initially she has only mild nausea and vomiting and otherwise feels well.
3. Over the next 1–2 days she develops right upper quadrant pain.
4. Laboratory studies show markedly elevated aminotransferases and a rising prothrombin time.
5. A nomogram plotting the drug level against time since ingestion predicts a high risk of liver injury.
6. Treatment with a glutathione-replenishing antidote is started.

TEACHING_POINTS

 A biphasic course with early mild symptoms followed by delayed liver injury suggests this common analgesic overdose.
 A toxic metabolite depletes hepatic glutathione and causes centrilobular necrosis.
 The Rumack-Matthew nomogram guides treatment based on level and timing.
 N-acetylcysteine replenishes glutathione and is most effective when given early.

DIAGNOSIS
Carbon monoxide poisoning

ALIASES
CO poisoning
Carbon monoxide toxicity
Carboxyhemoglobinemia

VIGNETTE_LINES

1. A family presents in winter with headache, dizziness, nausea, and confusion that improve when they leave the house.
2. They have been using a gas furnace and a charcoal heater indoors during a cold snap.
3. Several household members, including pets, are affected at the same time.
4. Examination reveals a normal oxygen saturation on standard pulse oximetry despite significant symptoms.
5. Arterial testing shows a normal PaO2 but an elevated abnormal-hemoglobin fraction on co-oximetry.
6. Treatment with 100% oxygen, and hyperbaric oxygen in severe cases, is initiated.

TEACHING_POINTS

 Headache, dizziness, and confusion affecting multiple household members in winter suggest an inhaled toxin.
 Standard pulse oximetry reads falsely normal because it cannot distinguish the abnormal hemoglobin.
 Co-oximetry measures the elevated carboxyhemoglobin fraction.
 Treatment is high-flow oxygen, with hyperbaric oxygen for severe cases.

CASE_ID 110
DIFFICULTY medium
SYSTEM pharmacology_toxicology

DIAGNOSIS
Organophosphate poisoning

ALIASES
Cholinesterase inhibitor toxicity
Anticholinesterase poisoning
Organophosphate toxicity

VIGNETTE_LINES

1. A 45-year-old farm worker presents with excessive salivation, tearing, and sweating after spraying crops.
2. He has blurred vision, abdominal cramps, diarrhea, and involuntary urination.
3. Examination reveals pinpoint pupils, wheezing with copious secretions, and muscle twitching.
4. He is bradycardic and diaphoretic, with a faint garlic-like odor.
5. Red-cell and plasma cholinesterase activity is markedly reduced.
6. Symptoms improve with atropine and a cholinesterase-reactivating agent.

TEACHING_POINTS

 Excessive secretions, miosis, bradycardia, and muscle fasciculations after pesticide exposure indicate cholinergic excess.
 The mnemonic SLUDGE (or DUMBELS) captures the muscarinic effects.
 Reduced cholinesterase activity confirms the diagnosis.
 Atropine blocks the muscarinic effects, while pralidoxime reactivates the enzyme if given early.
