CASE_ID: 001
DIFFICULTY: crimdle
SYSTEM: homicide

DIAGNOSIS:
First-Degree Murder

ALIASES:
premeditated murder
first degree murder
murder one

VIGNETTE_LINES:

1. A man learns over several weeks that his business partner has been quietly siphoning money from their company.
2. He stops confronting the partner and instead begins researching the partner's daily schedule and travel routes.
3. He purchases a firearm two towns away, pays cash, and tells the seller he wants something "that won't trace back."
4. On the chosen morning he waits in a parked car outside the partner's gym for nearly an hour before the partner arrives.
5. He approaches, fires a single shot to the head, and calmly walks back to his vehicle without taking the partner's wallet or watch.
6. At trial the jury is instructed that it must find a killing that was willful, deliberate, and premeditated to return the highest degree of the offense.

TEACHING_POINTS:

- First-degree murder requires a killing that is willful, deliberate, and premeditated; the planning and waiting period supply evidence of reflection.
- Premeditation can form quickly, but courts look for evidence of actual reflection (planning activity, motive, manner of killing).
- The absence of robbery negates a felony-murder theory and isolates the intentional, premeditated killing.
- Contrast with second-degree murder, which is intentional but lacks premeditation and deliberation.
- Procuring an untraceable weapon and surveilling the victim are classic "planning" and "manner" evidence of premeditation.

CASE_ID: 002
DIFFICULTY: crimdle
SYSTEM: homicide

DIAGNOSIS:
Depraved Heart Murder

ALIASES:
depraved heart murder
abandoned and malignant heart
second-degree reckless murder

VIGNETTE_LINES:

1. A man grows frustrated with cars speeding through his residential neighborhood late at night.
2. From an overpass, he begins dropping objects toward the roadway below to "scare" drivers into slowing down.
3. One night he heaves a chunk of broken concrete over the railing onto a busy four-lane highway.
4. He later admits he knew cars were passing constantly but says he never wanted to hit anyone.
5. The concrete crashes through a windshield and kills the driver instantly.
6. The prosecutor argues that the conduct showed an extreme indifference to the value of human life, supporting a murder conviction without proof of intent to kill.

TEACHING_POINTS:

- Depraved heart murder is an unintentional killing resulting from conduct showing extreme recklessness and indifference to human life.
- The mens rea is recklessness "plus": a gross deviation creating a very high risk of death, with conscious disregard.
- It is distinguished from involuntary manslaughter, which involves a lower degree of recklessness or criminal negligence.
- No intent to kill is required; the malice is implied from the wanton disregard for life.
- Classic examples include firing into a crowd, dropping heavy objects onto highways, and similar high-risk acts.

CASE_ID: 003
DIFFICULTY: crimdle
SYSTEM: homicide

DIAGNOSIS:
Felony Murder

ALIASES:
felony murder rule
felony-murder
FMR

VIGNETTE_LINES:

1. Two men plan to break into a jewelry store after closing, believing it will be empty.
2. One acts as lookout while the other forces the rear door and begins filling a bag with merchandise.
3. A night security guard they did not expect emerges and a struggle ensues.
4. During the scuffle the burglar's gun discharges, killing the guard, though both men later insist no one meant for anyone to die.
5. The lookout, who never entered the building or touched the weapon, is charged with the guard's death alongside his partner.
6. The court instructs that a death caused during the commission of an inherently dangerous felony supplies the malice for murder regardless of intent to kill.

TEACHING_POINTS:

- Felony murder imputes malice when a death occurs during the commission or attempted commission of a qualifying felony.
- Co-felons may be liable for deaths caused in furtherance of the felony, even without intent to kill or personal participation in the killing.
- Common predicate felonies include burglary, arson, rape, robbery, and kidnapping (often remembered as BARRK).
- The agency theory limits liability to deaths caused by the felons; the proximate cause theory is broader.
- The felony must be independent of the killing itself (the merger doctrine bars using assault as the predicate).

CASE_ID: 004
DIFFICULTY: crimdle
SYSTEM: homicide

DIAGNOSIS:
Voluntary Manslaughter

ALIASES:
voluntary manslaughter
heat of passion killing
provocation manslaughter

VIGNETTE_LINES:

1. A man returns home early from a work trip that was unexpectedly canceled.
2. He hears unfamiliar sounds from the bedroom and opens the door to find his spouse in bed with another person.
3. Enraged and shaking, he grabs a heavy lamp from the nightstand within seconds.
4. He strikes the other person repeatedly before either occupant can speak or dress.
5. The defense concedes the killing but argues the defendant acted before any reasonable opportunity to cool off.
6. The jury is instructed that adequate provocation that would inflame a reasonable person, with no cooling-off period, reduces what would otherwise be murder.

TEACHING_POINTS:

- Voluntary manslaughter is an intentional killing committed in the heat of passion upon adequate (legally sufficient) provocation.
- The provocation must be such as would inflame a reasonable person and must actually inflame the defendant.
- There must be no reasonable cooling-off period between the provocation and the killing.
- Discovery of adultery is a classic common law category of adequate provocation; mere words generally are not.
- It reduces murder to manslaughter by negating malice, not by excusing the killing entirely.

CASE_ID: 005
DIFFICULTY: crimdle
SYSTEM: homicide

DIAGNOSIS:
Involuntary Manslaughter

ALIASES:
involuntary manslaughter
criminally negligent homicide
unintentional manslaughter

VIGNETTE_LINES:

1. A babysitter is caring for a toddler and several other children on a hot summer afternoon.
2. She runs into a store to grab a few items, leaving the toddler buckled in the back seat with the windows up.
3. She becomes distracted by a phone call and a long checkout line, losing track of time.
4. Nearly forty minutes pass before she returns to the vehicle.
5. The toddler has died of hyperthermia, and the babysitter is hysterical and clearly never intended any harm.
6. The prosecution proceeds on a theory that her gross deviation from the standard of ordinary care caused the death.

TEACHING_POINTS:

- Involuntary manslaughter is an unintentional killing resulting from criminal negligence or during an unlawful act not amounting to a felony.
- Criminal negligence requires a gross deviation from the standard of care a reasonable person would observe.
- It differs from depraved heart murder in the degree of risk and culpability (negligence/lower recklessness vs extreme recklessness).
- The misdemeanor-manslaughter rule can supply liability when death occurs during a malum in se misdemeanor.
- Lack of intent to harm does not preclude liability where the conduct is grossly careless.

CASE_ID: 006
DIFFICULTY: crimdle
SYSTEM: inchoate_crimes

DIAGNOSIS:
Attempt

ALIASES:
criminal attempt
attempt
attempted crime

VIGNETTE_LINES:

1. A man decides to rob a bank he passes every day on his commute.
2. He buys a realistic toy pistol, writes a demand note, and studies the branch's opening hours.
3. On the planned morning he drives to the bank, parks, and walks toward the entrance with the note and pistol in his jacket.
4. As he reaches for the door handle, a passing patrol car slows nearby and he loses his nerve and leaves.
5. He is later arrested after the demand note and pistol are found and he confesses his plan.
6. The court must decide whether his conduct went beyond mere preparation and constituted a substantial step toward the offense.

TEACHING_POINTS:

- Attempt requires specific intent to commit the target offense plus an overt act beyond mere preparation.
- The MPC uses the "substantial step strongly corroborative of criminal purpose" test; common law often used proximity or other tests.
- Voluntary abandonment is a defense under the MPC if it is complete and a true renunciation, but not a defense at common law once the attempt is complete.
- Leaving because of an external deterrent (the patrol car) is generally not a voluntary, complete abandonment.
- Factual impossibility is no defense to attempt; legal impossibility traditionally is.

CASE_ID: 007
DIFFICULTY: crimdle
SYSTEM: inchoate_crimes

DIAGNOSIS:
Solicitation

ALIASES:
criminal solicitation
solicitation
incitement to crime

VIGNETTE_LINES:

1. A woman is locked in a bitter custody dispute with her former spouse.
2. At a bar she strikes up a conversation with a man she believes has a criminal reputation.
3. She offers him a sum of money and explains in detail that she wants her ex-spouse "taken care of permanently."
4. The man, who is actually an undercover officer, expresses interest but never agrees to do anything.
5. She hands over a cash deposit and a photograph of the intended victim.
6. The completed offense occurs the moment she requests the crime with intent that it be committed, regardless of whether anyone agrees or acts.

TEACHING_POINTS:

- Solicitation is asking, encouraging, or commanding another to commit a crime with the intent that the crime be committed.
- The offense is complete upon the request with the requisite intent; no agreement or further act is required.
- If the other person agrees, the crime may rise to conspiracy; if substantial steps follow, attempt may attach.
- Solicitation merges into the completed or attempted target offense (no conviction for both solicitation and the completed crime).
- It is irrelevant that the solicited party is an undercover officer who never intends to comply.

CASE_ID: 008
DIFFICULTY: crimdle
SYSTEM: inchoate_crimes

DIAGNOSIS:
Conspiracy

ALIASES:
criminal conspiracy
conspiracy
agreement to commit a crime

VIGNETTE_LINES:

1. Three coworkers at a shipping warehouse are unhappy with their pay.
2. Over several lunches they discuss how easy it would be to divert outgoing packages and resell the contents.
3. They agree on roles: one will alter shipping logs, one will load a private van, and one will handle resale.
4. One of them rents a storage unit and buys a set of box cutters and packing supplies for the scheme.
5. Before any package is taken, a fourth employee overhears them and reports the plan to management.
6. The prosecutor charges all three, arguing that the agreement plus an act in furtherance completes the offense even though no goods were ever stolen.

TEACHING_POINTS:

- Conspiracy is an agreement between two or more persons to commit an unlawful act, with intent to agree and intent to achieve the objective.
- Many jurisdictions and the MPC require an overt act in furtherance; the act may be lawful and need only be a step toward the goal.
- Conspiracy does not merge into the completed offense, so a defendant may be convicted of both.
- Under Pinkerton, co-conspirators are liable for reasonably foreseeable crimes committed in furtherance of the conspiracy.
- The unilateral approach (MPC) allows conspiracy even if only one party genuinely agrees; the bilateral approach requires two guilty minds.

CASE_ID: 009
DIFFICULTY: crimdle
SYSTEM: accomplice_liability

DIAGNOSIS:
Accomplice Liability

ALIASES:
aiding and abetting
accomplice liability
accessory before the fact

VIGNETTE_LINES:

1. A young man knows his friend intends to break into a rival's house to steal electronics.
2. He agrees to drive the friend to the neighborhood and wait nearby with the engine running.
3. He also lends the friend a crowbar and points out which window has a broken latch.
4. He never enters the house and takes none of the stolen property for himself.
5. The friend completes the break-in and theft while he waits in the car as planned.
6. The court holds him liable for the same offenses as the principal because he aided with the purpose of promoting the crime.

TEACHING_POINTS:

- An accomplice is one who, with the intent to promote or facilitate the offense, aids, encourages, or assists the principal.
- The accomplice is generally liable for the target crime and for other reasonably foreseeable crimes committed in its course.
- Mere presence or knowledge is insufficient; there must be intent to assist and an act of assistance.
- Modern law abolishes the principal/accessory-before-the-fact distinction, punishing accomplices as principals.
- Withdrawal can negate liability only if the accomplice neutralizes the assistance or gives timely notice before the crime.

CASE_ID: 010
DIFFICULTY: crimdle
SYSTEM: theft

DIAGNOSIS:
Larceny

ALIASES:
common law larceny
larceny
theft by taking

VIGNETTE_LINES:

1. A man is browsing in an electronics store and admires an expensive pair of headphones.
2. He looks around to confirm no employees are watching him near the display.
3. He slips the boxed headphones under his coat and conceals them against his body.
4. He walks several aisles away and toward the exit without approaching any register.
5. A loss-prevention officer stops him just past the last point of sale with the merchandise still hidden.
6. The prosecution emphasizes the trespassory taking and carrying away of another's property with intent to permanently deprive.

TEACHING_POINTS:

- Larceny is the trespassory taking and carrying away (asportation) of the personal property of another with intent to permanently deprive.
- Even slight movement satisfies asportation; concealment and movement toward an exit are strong evidence.
- The intent to permanently deprive must exist at the time of the taking (concurrence principle).
- Larceny is distinguished from embezzlement (lawful possession first) and false pretenses (title obtained by fraud).
- Taking property one believes is one's own, even mistakenly, can negate the intent to steal (claim of right).

CASE_ID: 011
DIFFICULTY: crimdle
SYSTEM: theft

DIAGNOSIS:
Embezzlement

ALIASES:
embezzlement
fraudulent conversion
theft by a fiduciary

VIGNETTE_LINES:

1. A bookkeeper is entrusted with managing a small nonprofit's bank accounts and writing its checks.
2. Facing personal debts, she begins writing checks to a shell vendor she secretly controls.
3. She records the payments as legitimate office expenses in the organization's ledgers.
4. Over a year she transfers tens of thousands of dollars she was lawfully authorized to handle into her own use.
5. An outside audit reveals the vendor does not exist and the funds went to her personal account.
6. The charge turns on the fact that she lawfully possessed the funds before fraudulently converting them to her own use.

TEACHING_POINTS:

- Embezzlement is the fraudulent conversion of property of another by a person already in lawful possession of it.
- The key distinction from larceny is that the defendant lawfully obtained possession before forming the intent to convert.
- A fiduciary or trust relationship typically gives rise to the lawful possession element.
- The conversion must be fraudulent and inconsistent with the owner's rights; mere borrowing with intent to return may not qualify.
- Falsifying records to conceal the conversion is strong evidence of fraudulent intent.

CASE_ID: 012
DIFFICULTY: crimdle
SYSTEM: theft

DIAGNOSIS:
False Pretenses

ALIASES:
false pretenses
theft by deception
obtaining property by false pretenses

VIGNETTE_LINES:

1. A man advertises a vintage car for sale that he claims has a rebuilt engine and clean title.
2. A buyer expresses interest, and the seller provides forged documents showing a recent engine overhaul.
3. He also knows, but conceals, that the car's title is salvaged after a prior flood.
4. Relying on these representations, the buyer pays full price and the seller signs over the vehicle.
5. The buyer later discovers the engine was never rebuilt and the title is worthless.
6. The prosecution stresses that the defendant obtained title to the money through a knowing false representation of material fact.

TEACHING_POINTS:

- False pretenses is obtaining title to property of another by a knowing or reckless false representation of a material past or present fact, with intent to defraud.
- The victim must actually rely on the misrepresentation, and the reliance must cause the transfer.
- The key distinction from larceny by trick is that false pretenses passes title, whereas larceny by trick passes only possession.
- A false promise of future conduct traditionally does not qualify; the misrepresentation must concern a past or present fact.
- Concealment of a material fact can constitute a misrepresentation in appropriate circumstances.

Robbery

ALIASES:
robbery
forcible theft
larceny by force

VIGNETTE_LINES:

CASE_ID: 014
DIFFICULTY: crimdle
SYSTEM: theft

DIAGNOSIS:
Burglary

ALIASES:
common law burglary
burglary
breaking and entering

VIGNETTE_LINES:

1. Late at night a man approaches a darkened single-family home he believes is occupied by a wealthy collector.
2. He finds a side window that is closed but unlocked and slides it open.
3. He climbs through into the living room without permission while the residents sleep upstairs.
4. He intends, once inside, to locate and take a rare coin collection he has heard about.
5. He is discovered by a resident before he finds anything and flees empty-handed.
6. The court explains that the offense was complete upon the unauthorized nighttime entry of a dwelling with intent to commit a felony inside.

TEACHING_POINTS:

- Common law burglary is the breaking and entering of the dwelling of another at night with intent to commit a felony therein.
- The intent to commit the felony must exist at the time of entry; later-formed intent does not satisfy common law burglary.
- "Breaking" can be actual (opening a closed window) or constructive (fraud, threat); opening an unlocked window counts, but a wide-open entry traditionally does not.
- Modern statutes often eliminate the nighttime and dwelling requirements and broaden "breaking."
- The crime is complete on entry with the requisite intent, regardless of whether the target felony is accomplished.

CASE_ID: 015
DIFFICULTY: crimdle
SYSTEM: theft

DIAGNOSIS:
Arson

ALIASES:
arson
malicious burning
common law arson

VIGNETTE_LINES:

1. A struggling restaurant owner is months behind on rent and facing eviction from his leased building.
2. He increases the fire insurance coverage on his business equipment shortly before the incident.
3. Late one night he spreads an accelerant through the kitchen and back office of the structure.
4. He ignites the accelerant and leaves, and the resulting fire chars the walls and ceiling of the building.
5. Investigators find pour patterns and accelerant residue inconsistent with an accidental fire.
6. The charge requires the malicious burning of a structure, and the charring of the building's fabric satisfies the burning element.

- "Malice" means intentional or reckless burning, not necessarily ill will.
- Burning one's own property to defraud an insurer is a common statutory form of arson.
- Insurance motive and accelerant evidence are circumstantial proof of malicious, intentional burning.

CASE_ID: 016
DIFFICULTY: crimdle
SYSTEM: defenses

DIAGNOSIS:
Self-Defense

ALIASES:
self-defense
justifiable use of force
perfect self-defense

VIGNETTE_LINES:

1. A man is confronted outside a bar by a larger, visibly intoxicated stranger who has been threatening patrons.
2. The stranger shoves him hard and shouts that he is going to "put him in the hospital."
3. The stranger then draws a knife and lunges toward the man's chest.
4. The man, who is unarmed, grabs a broken bottle from a nearby table.
5. He strikes once at the attacker, who dies from the wound, and the man immediately calls for help.
6. The jury is told that a defendant may use proportionate force, including deadly force, against a reasonable and imminent threat of death or serious bodily harm.

TEACHING_POINTS:

- Self-defense justifies force when the defendant reasonably believes it is necessary to repel an imminent unlawful threat.
- Deadly force is permitted only against a reasonable threat of death or serious bodily injury.
- The force used must be proportional to the threat faced.
- Many jurisdictions impose a duty to retreat before using deadly force (except in the home under the castle doctrine); stand-your-ground jurisdictions do not.
- A perfect self-defense claim, if accepted, results in acquittal because the killing is justified.

CASE_ID: 017
DIFFICULTY: crimdle
SYSTEM: defenses

DIAGNOSIS:
Imperfect Self-Defense

ALIASES:
imperfect self-defense
unreasonable self-defense
imperfect defense

VIGNETTE_LINES:

1. A man has received vague online threats from someone he has never met in person.
2. One evening a stranger approaches him in a parking lot reaching into a jacket pocket.
3. Genuinely terrified and convinced this is the person who threatened him, the man pulls a handgun.
4. He fires, killing the stranger, who turns out to have been reaching for a phone to call for directions.
5. The jury finds the man honestly believed he was in danger, but that his belief was unreasonable under the circumstances.
6. The court explains that this honest-but-unreasonable belief does not justify the killing but reduces it from murder to manslaughter.

TEACHING_POINTS:

- Imperfect self-defense applies when the defendant honestly but unreasonably believes deadly force is necessary.
- It does not justify the killing but negates malice, reducing murder to voluntary manslaughter in many jurisdictions.
- It can also arise where the defendant was the initial aggressor or used excessive force.
- Contrast with perfect self-defense, which requires a reasonable belief and results in acquittal.
- Not all jurisdictions recognize imperfect self-defense; where they do, it is a partial, mitigating defense.

CASE_ID: 018
DIFFICULTY: crimdle
SYSTEM: defenses

DIAGNOSIS:
Insanity Defense

ALIASES:
insanity defense
not guilty by reason of insanity
M'Naghten rule

VIGNETTE_LINES:

1. A man with a long psychiatric history stops taking his prescribed medication for several weeks.
2. He becomes convinced that his neighbor is a demonic entity sent to harvest his soul.
3. Acting on this belief, he attacks and kills the neighbor in broad daylight in front of witnesses.
4. When arrested he calmly explains that he "destroyed a demon" and saved his own life.
5. Multiple experts agree that due to his mental disease he could not understand that his act was wrong.
6. The verdict turns on whether, because of a mental disease or defect, he lacked the capacity to know the nature of his act or that it was wrong.

TEACHING_POINTS:

- The M'Naghten test asks whether, due to mental disease or defect, the defendant could not know the nature and quality of the act or that it was wrong.
- The irresistible impulse test adds a volitional prong: whether the defendant could control conduct.
- The MPC test combines cognitive and volitional prongs (lacking substantial capacity to appreciate criminality or conform conduct).
- The Durham (product) test asks whether the act was the product of mental illness; it is now largely abandoned.
- Insanity is an excuse defense; a successful claim yields not guilty by reason of insanity, often with civil commitment.

CASE_ID: 019
DIFFICULTY: crimdle
SYSTEM: defenses

DIAGNOSIS:
Duress

ALIASES:
duress
coercion defense
compulsion

VIGNETTE_LINES:

1. A bank teller is approached outside work by men who show him photographs of his children at school.
2. They tell him that unless he helps them, his family will be harmed that very day.
3. The next morning they wait outside while he disables an alarm and opens a side door for them.
4. He participates in the resulting theft only while the men remain in contact and watching him.
5. He reports everything to police the moment the threat to his family is over.
6. The defense argues he should be excused because he reasonably believed he faced an imminent threat of serious harm with no safe escape.

TEACHING_POINTS:

- Duress excuses criminal conduct (other than intentional murder at common law) when the defendant acts under an imminent threat of death or serious bodily harm.
- The threat must be of imminent harm to the defendant or another, with no reasonable opportunity to escape.
- Duress is generally not a defense to intentional homicide, though it may mitigate.
- It is an excuse defense (the actor is not blameworthy), distinct from necessity (a justification based on choosing the lesser evil).
- The threat must come from a human source; threats from natural forces implicate necessity instead.

CASE_ID: 020
DIFFICULTY: crimdle
SYSTEM: defenses

DIAGNOSIS:
Necessity

ALIASES:
necessity
choice of evils
lesser harm defense

VIGNETTE_LINES:

1. A sudden wildfire spreads rapidly toward a small mountain community with only one road out.
2. A group of hikers becomes trapped as flames cut off their planned route.
3. To survive, they break into a locked, unoccupied cabin to shelter from the smoke and heat.
4. Inside, they use the owner's food, water, and radio without permission.
5. They are later charged with breaking and entering and theft of the supplies.
6. The defense argues their conduct should be justified because they reasonably chose a lesser harm to avoid imminent natural danger.

TEACHING_POINTS:

- Necessity justifies otherwise criminal conduct when it is reasonably taken to avoid a greater imminent harm, usually from natural forces.
- The harm avoided must be greater than the harm caused (the choice-of-evils balance).
- The defendant must not have created the emergency and must have no reasonable lawful alternative.
- Necessity is a justification (the act is socially desirable), whereas duress is an excuse based on human coercion.
- At common law, necessity traditionally was not a defense to murder.

voluntary intoxication

diminished capacity by intoxication

CASE_ID: 022
DIFFICULTY: crimdle
SYSTEM: defenses

DIAGNOSIS:
Infancy

ALIASES:
infancy defense
infancy
juvenile incapacity

VIGNETTE_LINES:

1. A six-year-old takes a neighbor's bicycle from an open garage and rides it home.
2. The child keeps the bicycle and tells friends it now belongs to him.
3. The neighbor reports the bicycle missing and it is traced to the child's home.
4. Prosecutors consider whether the child can be held criminally responsible for a theft offense.

- Modern statutes vary, often setting minimum ages and channeling juveniles into the juvenile justice system.
- Infancy is an incapacity defense focused on the inability to form the requisite mens rea.

CASE_ID: 023
DIFFICULTY: crimdle
SYSTEM: defenses

DIAGNOSIS:
Entrapment

ALIASES:
entrapment
government inducement
entrapment defense

VIGNETTE_LINES:

1. A man with no criminal record is befriended by an undercover agent over several months.
2. The agent repeatedly pressures him to obtain and sell a controlled substance, which the man initially refuses.
3. The agent appeals to sympathy, claims to be sick, and asks again and again until the man relents.
4. The man eventually acquires the drug solely to satisfy the persistent requests and hands it to the agent.

6. The defense argues the criminal design originated with the government and that the defendant was not predisposed to commit the offense.

TEACHING_POINTS:

- Merely providing an opportunity to commit a crime (e.g., a sting) is not entrapment.
- Predisposition can be shown by ready compliance, prior conduct, or expertise; persistent inducement of a reluctant, unpredisposed person supports the defense.

5. The bystander dies, while the intended victim walks away unaware of the attempt.
6. The court holds the defendant liable for murder of the bystander because his intent to kill follows the bullet to the unintended victim.

TEACHING_POINTS:

- Transferred intent applies the defendant's intent toward an intended victim to an unintended victim who is actually harmed.
- It typically operates within the same type of harm and the same general crime (intent to kill transfers to a killing).
- The defendant may also be liable for attempt as to the intended victim, depending on the jurisdiction.
- The doctrine does not transfer across different social harms (e.g., intent to break a window does not become intent to injure a person in all jurisdictions).
- It is most commonly applied in homicide and battery contexts.
