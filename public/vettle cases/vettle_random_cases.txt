DIFFICULTY: navle
SYSTEM: small_animal_internal_medicine
DIAGNOSIS:
Diabetic Ketoacidosis
ALIASES:
DKA
complicated diabetes mellitus
VIGNETTE_LINES:
1. An 8-year-old dog presents for several days of vomiting, weakness, and collapse on a background of recent polyuria and polydipsia.
2. The owner noticed weight loss despite a good appetite over the prior month but had not sought care.
3. Exam reveals dehydration, tachypnea, obtundation, and a faintly sweet breath odor.
4. Differentials for the acute crisis include pancreatitis and renal failure; a venous blood gas shows a high-anion-gap metabolic acidosis.
5. Bloodwork reveals marked hyperglycemia, glucosuria, ketonuria, and a low potassium once fluids are started.
6. Treatment is staged: isotonic fluids and potassium first, then a regular insulin CRI, with a search for the precipitating illness.
TEACHING_POINTS:
- DKA is decompensated diabetes with ketonemia and a high-anion-gap metabolic acidosis from unchecked lipolysis and ketogenesis.
- A concurrent illness (pancreatitis, infection, hyperadrenocorticism, diestrus) usually precipitates the crisis; always look for it.
- Restore volume and correct potassium (and phosphate) before/while giving insulin; total-body potassium is depleted even if serum K is initially normal.
- Use short-acting regular insulin during stabilization; avoid lowering glucose faster than fluids/electrolytes can keep up.
- Monitor for hypokalemia, hypophosphatemia (hemolysis), hypoglycemia, and cerebral edema during correction.

DIFFICULTY: navle
SYSTEM: small_animal_toxicology
DIAGNOSIS:
Anticoagulant Rodenticide Toxicosis
ALIASES:
warfarin toxicity
vitamin K antagonist rodenticide
VIGNETTE_LINES:
1. A young dog presents for acute lethargy, exercise intolerance, and labored breathing.
2. The owner found chewed bait packaging in the garage several days earlier but didn't think the dog ate any.
3. Exam reveals pale mucous membranes and muffled ventral lung sounds, with no petechiae or external bleeding.
4. Differentials include immune-mediated disease and trauma; a coagulation panel shows markedly prolonged PT and aPTT with a normal platelet count.
5. Thoracic imaging confirms pleural/pulmonary hemorrhage (a cavity bleed) rather than mucosal bleeding.
6. Vitamin K1 is initiated, with plasma/whole-blood transfusion for the acute bleed, and the K1 duration is tailored to the anticoagulant generation.
TEACHING_POINTS:
- Anticoagulant rodenticides inhibit vitamin K epoxide reductase, depleting factors II, VII, IX, and X.

- Bleeding is typically into cavities/spaces (hemothorax, hemoabdomen, hemarthrosis), unlike the petechiae of platelet disorders.
- Treatment is vitamin K1 (oral once stable) plus FFP or whole blood for active life-threatening hemorrhage.
- Second-generation products (e.g., brodifacoum) require longer K1 courses (often 3-4 weeks) with a PT recheck after stopping.

ALIASES:
antifreeze poisoning
ethylene glycol intoxication
VIGNETTE_LINES:

- Cats are exquisitely sensitive with a very short window; once anuric AKI develops, prognosis is grave.

5. Whole-body radiographs reveal metallic densities in the GI tract, and elevated blood zinc/lead confirms heavy metal toxicosis.
6. Treatment includes chelation (e.g., CaEDTA), supportive care, and removal/retrieval of the metal source.
TEACHING_POINTS:
- Heavy metal toxicosis (lead, zinc) is common in parrots that chew galvanized metal, solder, paint, or jewelry.
- Signs are nonspecific: GI (regurgitation, green droppings/biliverdinuria), hematologic, and neurologic (ataxia, seizures).
- Radiographs often reveal metallic foreign material in the GI tract; confirm with blood lead/zinc levels.
- Treatment is chelation (CaEDTA, sometimes DMSA) with supportive care and removing the metal source.
- Prevention is "bird-proofing" the environment (avoid galvanized wire, lead weights, and accessible metals).

- Treatment addresses water quality and stocking plus antibacterials; differentiate from true fungal (Saprolegnia) infection and ich.
